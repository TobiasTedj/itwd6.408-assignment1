COPYRIGHT, CREATIVE COMMON (CC) LICENSES, FAIR USE

Copyright is a form of intellectual property right. It gives the person who creates an original work exclusive rights to copy, publish, publicly perform, transmit and adapt their material. [1]

Copyright applies automatically from when your work is created/published, and, in most circumstances, lasts for your lifetime plus 50 years. [2]

Creative Commons (CC) is a non-profit organisation that enables sharing and reuse of creators works through legal means. CC Licenses are copyright licenses with standardised terms developed by CC that allow creators who want to share their work to do so, while still retaining some rights. [3]

Examples of CC Licenses: [4]

  • CC BY: Credit must be given to the author.
  • CC BY-SA: Credit must be given to the author. Adaptations must be shared under the same terms.
  • CC BY-NC: Credit must be given to the author. Only noncommercial uses of the work are permitted.
  • CC BY-ND: Credit must be given to the author. No derivatives or adaptations of the work are permitted.

"Fair Use" does not apply within NZ. The term "Fair Use" is an American term within an American Application. But NZ does have a similar equivalent called "Fair Dealing", however, it is more strict in terms of its definition and application. Both deal with the use of copyrighted works but while "Fair Use" can be quite vague and each case of copyright infringement under the argument of "Fair Use" must be examined on a case-by-case basis, "Fair Dealing" is a stricter set of guidelines on what copyrighted material can and cannot be used without the express permission of the copyright holder. [5]

PRIVACY AND PRIVACY POLICIES

A privacy policy is a legal document that explains how an organization handles any customer, client or employee information gathered in its operations. [1]

Some of the things a privacy policy can include:

  • What is considered personal information.
  • Types of data collected - name, date of birth, location etc.
  • How data is collected.
  • How the data will be used.
  • If the data will be disclosed internally.
  • If the data will be disclosed externally to third-parties.
  • Cookies and how their used
  • How the data will be stored and protected.
  • How to opt out and request deletion.
  • Date the policy comes into effect.
  • Contact information for privacy-related requests.
  • List of user rights in regards to personal information collected.
  • How changes to the privacy policy will be communicated.

WEBSITE SEO

SEO stands for Search Engine Optimisation. It describes designs, implementations and techniques that improve your websites visibility in search engine results e.g. where it places on a Google search result page. Better SEO means that search engines are able to more easily identify the content in your website and what it has to offer. This is extremely important when trying to grow your audience and potential customers because when people search for a topic, content, service etc. online, most people only take into consideration the top few results on the result page. Better SEO means search engines have a more accurate understanding of your website and can relay that information better to search engine users.

5 ways to improve SEO:

  1. Organise your website: For example, make sure HTML tags are being implemented correctly. When search engines analyse your site, they use things like header tags to determine the relevance and importance of content on your site.
  2. Keywords: Keep in mind what kind of search terms users may input and use that as a basis for determining keywords to include in your website. This will make your website content more relevant to those you want to target.
  3. Optomise for mobile: Ensure your website is mobile-responsive and offers a seamless user experience on smartphones and tablets
  4. Metadata: Webpages can contain metadata between the <head> tags. Metadata carries information about the contents of your page, such as title, description etc. It is important this metadata is accurate for search engines as this is also used to determine relevancy of content.
  5. Alt tags: Make sure any media on your website has alt tags/alternative text descriptions. This is the short description of the image or video on your webpage that is used by search engines to determine what it is actually showing, since they are unable to determine what the image or video is without a description. When users search for an image of something, search engines know exactly what the images on your website is because of the alt tag and can then show that to user if it is relevant.

WEB HOSTING/PUBLISHING

5 factors to consider when choosing a web hosting service:
  1. Reliability and Uptime: Look for a provider with a good record of stability, reliability and offers a high uptime guarantee (>99%). This ensures that your website will be available to visitors effectively at all times.
  2. Speed: Look for a provider that offers good loading speed. This not only improves user experience for visitors, but relates back to SEO, as search engines like Google prioritise faster loading websites.
  3. Customer Support: Ensure that the provider has a good customer support reputation. Look for things like 24/7 support and/or multiple contact channels (live chat, phone, email). When something does go wrong with your website, troubleshooting quickly is important to minimise downtime as much as possible.
  4. Scalability: Depending on your intentions for your website, you may want to choose a provider that is able to scale and grow with your needs in the future. Look for a provider that can not only meet that growth demand, but makes it easy to upgrade in the future.
  5. Data Integrity and Security: Make sure the provider you choose provides security features such as traffic encryption between your website and visitors. Features like a backup service is important for making sure that recovery is possible in case of data loss or similar issues.

WEB PERFORMANCE AND MAINTENANCE

5 things to do to improve website performance and reduce page loading time:
  1. Optimise images: Altering the format and dimensions of images on your website can decrease their file size and improve their load times.
  2. Minimise HTTP requests: Every time a page loads up, it needs to download components of that page (e.g. images, stylesheets, scripts). Minimising the amount of things you need to download will reduce your load times. For example, where possible, you can combine stylesheets to reduce the number of requests and downloads.
  3. Use HTTP caching: Store static files such as Javascript and CSS in the browsers cache so that they can load recently visited websites faster.
  4. Optimise JavaScript and CSS: JavaScript and CSS should be stored in external files so they can be stored in the cache for faster loading. Also ensure that you take advantage of asynchronous loading. JS and CSS files can load asynchronously, meaning that while your website makes a request for files, it won't have to wait for the reply before moving on and doing the next thing it needs to do. It will be able to load multiple files at the same time, speeding up the page's performance.
  5. Limit the use of external scripts: Every time a page loads, any scripted webpage elements from and external source need to be loaded as well. Depending on the size, these can slow a webpage down.
5 things to do for web maintenance:
  1. Backups: Make regular backups of your website. Make your own backps on top of any backup service provided by the web host provider.
  2. Keep your website updated: Make sure content on your website is always as relevant and up-to-date as possible. This helps with SEO and encourages visitors to return and use your website.
  3. Maintain links: If you have any links on your website to third parties/external services, make sure they still work after setting it up. Links can go dead for various reasons so regular check that they all still work as intended.
  4. Test browser compatibility: Browsers are constantly being updated with new standards and features. Regularly check your website is still compatible with as many different browsers and browser versions as possible.
  5. Review analytics and statistics: Keep track of your websites performance and traffic to gain insight on how your website is performing and if there are any areas you can improve on.

WEB SECURITY

5 common cyber atacks:
  1. Malware: Malware is any code or programme designed to damage or control a computer system. There are many forms of malware such as ransomware, spyware and trojans. These are all designed to harm your system, whether that's restricting functionality or stealing data.
  2. Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks: A DoS attack is a targeted attack that inundates a server or network with an extremely large amount of requests, overwhelming the system. As the system tries to handle the number of requests, resources are entirely used up and real requests become impossible to resolve, disrupting operations. This costs businesses time and money to resolve. A Distributed Denial-of-Service attack (DDoS) is the same as DoS attack but instead of the fake requests coming from one source, the system is attacked from multiple sources. This makes it harder to resolve the attack as multiple sources need to be blocked for normal operations to resume.
  3. Phishing: Phishing is a cyber attack that tries to trick victims into downloading malware or sharing sensitive information such as log in credentials, account numbers etc. There are many types of phishing scams, but most commonly the phisher will pose as person or entity such as a bank or postal company and contact the victim via email, sms, phone etc. They will ask them to click on a link to "reset their password", download an "important file" or "claim a missing package", all of which could ultimately lead the victim into mistakenly downloading a virus or share sensitive information.
  4. Spoofing: Similar to phishing, this cyber attack involves a malicious party posing as someone the victim knows to lull them into a false sense of security. They can pose as coworkers, friends or family members. The attacker will try and get the victim to divulge sensitive information, send them money or give them access to the victims devices remotely.
  5. Code injection attacks: Attackers will try to inject malicious code into vulnerable systems and applications to alter its behaviour and functionality. For example, an SQL injection takes advantage of system vulnerabilities to inject SQL statemnets into the system to try and compromise the systems database. This can result in altered, lost or stolen information.
